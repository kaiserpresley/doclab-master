Genetics and Inheritance of Sickle Cell Anemia

Sickle cell anemia is an inherited blood disorder caused by a mutation in the HBB gene, which provides instructions for making hemoglobin. Hemoglobin is a protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen throughout the body. The mutation causes the hemoglobin to form into an abnormal shape, which leads to the characteristic sickle shape of the red blood cells.

Inheritance:

Sickle cell anemia is an autosomal recessive disorder, which means that a person must inherit two copies of the mutated HBB gene, one from each parent, to develop the disease. If a person inherits only one copy of the mutated gene and one normal gene, they will have sickle cell trait, which is usually asymptomatic.

The inheritance pattern of sickle cell anemia can be illustrated using a Punnett square, which is a diagram that predicts the probability of offspring inheriting certain traits based on the genotypes of their parents.

As shown in the Punnett square, if both parents have sickle cell trait, there is a 25% chance that their child will inherit two copies of the mutated gene and develop sickle cell anemia.

It is important for individuals with sickle cell anemia or sickle cell trait to inform their family members about their condition and the risk of inheritance. Genetic counseling can also help families understand the inheritance pattern of sickle cell anemia and make informed decisions about family planning.
